Reading note: SLI/SLO/SLA

Bryant Tseng
2023-05-05
Note, SRE

Background

Reading the Chapter 4 - Service Level Objectives of Google SRE Book.

I found it important to hava a good understanding of what SLI/SLO/SLA is.

We often heard SLA a lot.
But sometimes (including me) is easy to misuse it with SLO, so I decided to take a note here so that I can review this in the future.

Disclaimer

In this note. The termilogy/definition are most coming from the Google SRE Book.
Despite I tend to agree most of the case being describe in the book.
I still believe that there is no one-fit-all silver bullet for all usecases.

Definition

Let’s see the Definition of SLI/SLO/SLA are being describe in the Book.

SLI - Service Level Indicator

An SLI is a service level indicator — a carefully defined quantitative measure of some aspect of the level of service that is provided.

Which means, a SLI should be something that can indicate how your service is working.

for example:

  • latency of the api endpoint.
  • time for a periodic job to finish

Keep in mind that the SLI here is not defining whether it’s a good or bad situation.
It’s just providing an indicator so that the observer can later decide how the service is running.

SLO - Service Level Objective

An SLO is a service level objective: a target value or range of values for a service level that is measured by an SLI.

Which means, a SLO is defining the target of the service should be running base on the SLI.

for example:

  • The service has SLI which is the api http-request latency,
    A SLO is set for the service that the service is targeting to servce the http request under 100ms of latency.

Here the SLO is basically setting the line between a servcie which is running as expected and the one which is not.

A single service can have multuple SLOs. Which in my opinion can help observer to have a better understanding when checking on the service.

SLA - Service Level Agreement

An SLA is an explicit or implicit contract with your users that includes consequences of meeting (or missing) the SLOs they contain.

Which means, a SLA is a agreement between the service and it’s user. Alongside with it’s consequences.

for example:

  • The service has a SLA to have 99.99% uptime to it’s user. which means the service can have up to (365*24*60*0.01)=87.60 hrs downtime across the year.
    In which whether or not the service is up is defined by the SLO.
    If the agreement is not met.The service’s owner need to have some form of compensate to it’s user.

Misuse between SLA and SLO

We often heard that a specific service has a SLA of 4-nines(which means 99.99%) to it’s uptime.
But despite reading or hearing that, the correspond SLO is not mentioned.

This makes the SLA ambiguous.
In what circumstance I can consider this service is up?, is a very slow but success request response be considered up service?

Or another case is that, SLA is defined but the consequences of breaking the SLA is not defined.
Which make the SLA meaningless. Afterall, a SLA is a Agreement.
Breaking a agreement should come which a consequence.

In the case if the consequence is no needed(more often the internal service which is not directly customer facing), simply define the SLO is enough.
Try to keep the service to meet a defined target - which is SLO.
